# Revised Design Manifesto

This pack is not a tech tree.
It is an **industrial fantasy world** where progress is earned by mastering matter, distance, mortality, and place.

The graph is not meant to represent recipe completeness.
It is meant to represent the **real stories of expansion** that settlements tell about themselves.

A player should be able to say:

- we got the meltery up
- we have real rail now
- steel is online
- slurries are stable
- the grid holds
- we can support obelisk operations
- we can keep a remnant lattice running
- respectable magic is supplied
- we can survive off-world
- we can synthesize what geology no longer gives freely

That is the scale that matters.

---

## 1. Progress must feel like conquest, not checklisting

A new tier should not feel like:

> mine next ore, smelt next ingot, craft next machine.

A new tier should feel like:

> find the new thing, survive the place that contains it, master the transformation that makes it useful, and only then earn the right to build with it.

That is the governing law of the pack:

## **new resource domain + signature transformation = new tier**

The transformation might be alloying, bloodwork, dragon-kill access, obelisk sustainment, space industry, slurry chemistry, or synthesis.
The important part is not that processing gets more complicated.
The important part is that processing gets more **meaningful**.

---

## 2. Matter should have memory

Ore is not just ore.
A deposit is a **reservoir of hidden structure**.

Early on, players extract the obvious material.
Later, they separate alloying elements, toxic traces, precious inclusions, or fissile components.
Eventually, they stop treating the earth as a pile of ingots and start treating it as a **chemical inheritance** that can be traversed, decomposed, and synthesized.

That is why the ore graph is staged.
Later outputs are not “bonus yield.”
They are a record of how deeply the player has learned to interrogate the world.

Early game is crude extraction.
Midgame is intelligent separation.
Late game is deliberate traversal of material space.

That is why **sophisticated slurries** and **advanced synthesis** are central.
They are not efficiency upgrades.
They are epistemic upgrades.

---

## 3. Death is the universal magical substrate

Tech interrogates the earth.
Magic interrogates what remains.

The magical foundation of the pack is not floating mana, ambient purity, or abstract spell power.
It is the **persistent metaphysical residue of ended lives**.

On death, a **Remnant** condenses: a bound residue containing aspects of the life, death, place, and condition of the deceased.

That remnant is not a clean magical item.
It is singular, unstable, and overdetermined.
Different magical traditions interpret it differently.

- Blood Magic sees sacrifice, vitality, violence, and price.
- Malum sees spirit persistence and soul resonance.
- Eidolon sees necro-alchemical matter and grave significance.
- Occultism sees bound spiritual medium and other-side labor.
- Ars Nouveau sees attunable magical imprint.
- Hex sees informational structure that can be abstracted into pattern.

So the correct statement is not:

> some magic is death magic.

The correct statement is:

> all advanced magic is downstream of death, but not all schools are equally honest about it.

---

## 4. Some schools are substrate-native, and some are reformist

The pack’s magical traditions do not all stand at the same distance from mortality.

### Death-native schools
These read raw remnants directly.
They are close to sacrifice, spirit, grave matter, and the other side.

- Blood Magic
- Malum
- Eidolon
- Occultism

These schools do the first ugly work.
They know how to bind, render, formalize, stabilize, and standardize remnants into usable magical intermediates.

### Reformist schools
These are formal, respectable, generalized, or abstracted magical practices.
They prefer translated magical matter rather than raw postmortem substrate.

- Ars Nouveau
- Hex Casting
- Mana and Artifice
- Mahou Tsukai
- related ritualized or institutional schools

These schools are not fake.
They are valid.
They are simply **downstream**.
They live in a world whose magical economy has already been made legible by death-native arts.

That is an important distinction.
The reformists are not frauds.
They are politically cleaner than their supply chain.

---

## 5. Remnants should be interpreted, not processed like ore

The pack should not turn death-substrate into a fake industrial clone of metallurgy.
A remnant is not an ingot path.

A remnant should remain a **single captured specimen** whose stored contents determine how it can be used.
The interesting part is not processing it into increasingly refined remnant items.
The interesting part is that its recorded qualities make it suitable for different magical roles.

A remnant can be used in three ways:

### 1. As a non-consuming catalyst
A remarkable remnant can anchor a magical line without being consumed.
This is the preferred use of premium specimens.

### 2. As a whole-remnant expenditure
Some breakthroughs should demand a real and irreversible sacrifice.
This is where apex crafts live.

### 3. As fractionation stock
A remnant can be broken down into lesser magical materials for smaller crafts and routine work.
This is the fallback path.

The remnant is not valuable because it went through the correct workstation.
It is valuable because of **what happened**.

---

## 6. Great remnants should found industries

Mass production should not mean farming mountains of interchangeable corpse-items.
That would flatten the fantasy.

Instead:

- one exceptional remnant becomes an **anchor specimen**
- lesser remnants keep that anchor viable
- ordinary reagents and infrastructure feed the process
- ongoing output is produced because the anchor remains in place

That is the correct magical-industrial distinction.

Tech consumes feedstocks.
Magic uses **qualified remains** to determine what can be transformed.

This is why the **Remnant Lattice** matters.
A high-quality remnant is not just stored.
It is **kept alive in operation**.
Poorer remnants are then fed into the lattice as maintenance mass, sacrificial circulation, stability ballast, or replacement pulse.

Good remnants found industries.
Bad remnants keep them beating.

That is the economy.

---

## 7. The magical supply chain should remain visible

A major design risk is letting every school consume raw remnants equally.
If that happens, the magical economy flattens into a universal token system.

That would be a mistake.

Instead, raw remnants should mainly belong to death-native traditions.
Those traditions then yield **standardized magical intermediates** such as spirit residue, necromantic salts, mnemonic ash, sacrificial concentrates, bound echoes, and related materials.

Reformist schools should consume those translated products far more often than raw remnants themselves.

That way the player can eventually learn the uncomfortable truth:

> respectable magic did not replace death magic.
> respectable magic depends on it.

That is the ideological structure of the pack.

---

## 8. Place must matter

Important things must exist **somewhere**.

That is why:

- ore bodies have geological identity
- mountains matter
- meteors matter
- obelisks matter
- evil biomes matter
- off-world sites matter
- deep underworld access matters
- dimension entry points and remote production sites should not collapse into one bunker

The pack should reward:

- discovering a place
- securing a place
- routing to a place
- industrializing a place
- defending a place over time

Not just holding the correct item.

A space access point that cannot simply be abstracted away is good design.
A rare mountain material that must be locally exploited is good design.
A dangerous permanent outpost is good design.

This pack should feel territorial.

---

## 9. Logistics is not quality-of-life, logistics is truth

A world without trivial teleportation is honest.

If matter matters, moving it must matter.
If sites matter, connecting them must matter.
If scale matters, freight must matter.

That is why trains are not decoration.
That is why boat access, route control, and remote-site servicing matter.
That is why local AE2 is good while world-erasing logistics is bad.

The player should be able to read a mature settlement in its routes:

- rail spines
- depots
- outposts
- local processing nodes
- distributed magical supply
- climate-support staging
- remote integration of dangerous sites

Progress should be measurable in how much of the world has been made **legible and circulatory**.

---

## 10. Electricity should feel engineered, not generic

Force and heat do the ordinary work of industry.
Electricity is not the everyday universal substrate.
It is the advanced, formalized, engineered layer that compresses complexity and unlocks harder systems.

Electricity should feel like:

- discipline
- abstraction
- infrastructural maturity
- deliberate elegance

It should not erase the physical world too early.
It should not trivialize force and heat.
It should represent the point where industry becomes more exacting, more formal, and more infrastructural.

That is why **Grid Power** is a major gate.
Not because everything should instantly become RF-shaped, but because the pack should eventually ask for genuine engineering.

---

## 11. AE2 should unify systems, not annihilate geography

Early and local AE2 is good because it lets intelligence spread without making the map irrelevant.
A site can become smart without ceasing to be local.

The Controller is the serious reunification point:

- advanced tech
- advanced magic materials
- stable industrial maturity
- broad logistical competence
- a settlement sophisticated enough to abstract some complexity without erasing the world

That is the correct role for AE2 here.
It should unify systems, not flatten places.

---

## 12. Side systems must justify themselves economically

A side system belongs in the pack only if it enters the real grammar of progress.
That means:

- boss loot becomes a real reagent or gate
- villagers matter because they provide parallel leverage
- nutrition matters because bodies are infrastructure
- climate matters because survivability expands operational range
- exploration matters because first access and territorial knowledge matter
- magical schools matter because they occupy distinct positions in the supply chain

Nothing gets to stay just because it is “cool.”
It has to answer:

> what kind of leverage does this give the player or settlement?

If the answer is good enough, it belongs.
If not, it stays ornamental.

---

## 13. Rackets are not classes, they are lies about prosperity

Starting rackets should not be combat classes or lore costumes.
They are **economic entry stories**.
Each one claims to be the master variable of civilization.

- the machinist believes throughput solves everything
- the provisioner believes bodies are the real infrastructure
- the ritualist believes proper magic is civilization
- the surveyor believes access is sovereignty
- the outfitter believes survivability determines who can do business at all

Each racket is partially right.
Each racket is also seductively incomplete.

That is the point.
A good start is not “you are a different hero.”
A good start is “you begin the game inside a plausible economic delusion.”

---

## 14. The graph should privilege milestones over recipes

The graph exists to show major historical moments in the life of a settlement, not every machine recipe.

A good node is something players will actually say to each other.

- we have the meltery
- we can move freight now
- steel is online
- the grid is stable
- we can keep the lattice fed
- obelisk operations are sustainable
- local AE2 is working
- controller is online
- post-space is open
- synthesis is live

That is the correct scale.
Recipes are implementation details.
Milestones are the story.

---

## 15. Endgame must be expansive, not terminal

The goal is not to reach a node labeled “done.”
The goal is to reach a point where all the pack’s systems become material for larger ambitions.

Late game should feel like:

- maintaining dangerous territories
- scaling distributed civilization
- industrializing off-world sites
- sustaining the remnant lattice at scale
- running obelisk operations as infrastructure
- synthesizing what geology withholds
- building projects that only a mature society could afford

Endgame should feel like **power with obligations**.
Not a finished staircase.

---

# The design creed

This pack believes that:

- progress should be earned by mastery, not by memorizing recipes
- the world should remain physical
- distance should remain costly
- ore should remain deep
- death should remain meaningful
- magic should remain politically divided over its own substrate
- logistics should remain civilizational
- electricity should remain engineered
- AE2 should remain local until maturity
- endgame should remain hungry for ambition

This is not a balanced staircase of unlocks.
It is a world where every new tier should feel like the result of a society learning how to do something it previously had no right to do.
